MapPin'

Robotics Accessibility Embedded Systems 3D Printing

A visual, tactile, and auditory map to aid people who struggle with dyslexia and visual impairments.

Problem

Dyslexia, a condition that usually affects reading and writing, also hinders spatial awareness, especially with maps. People with dyslexia struggle to convert 2D images on maps into 3D spatial direction in real life. This difficulty arises from differences in how their brains process visual and spatial signals. Despite extensive research, there's still no widely available solution, prompting my efforts to develop one.

My Proposed Solution

I’m developing a device inspired by a 3D Pin-art toy to help people with dyslexia navigate more effectively.

Each pin will be individually controllable, allowing for tactile interaction with a 3D map displayed on a flexible OLED screen, similar to Samsung’s foldable phone displays. The device will combine tactile, visual, and auditory elements to create a multisensory navigation experience.

A companion app will handle GPS functions and heavy calculations, allowing the device to stay lightweight. The app will include general city navigation and specialized features for digitizing maps of theme parks, parks, and other areas.

Expert Feedback

I recently presented my MapPin' project to Mrs. Valerie Rapowitz, who was excited about its potential to improve navigation for people with dyslexia. She encouraged me to present it at the International Dyslexia Association’s upcoming conference in Dallas. She also pointed out that the device could benefit not only dyslexics but also individuals with speech impediments who struggle with navigation, as well as those who are visually impaired. With her feedback regarding auditory inputs and tactile value for ADHD users, I plan to refine MapPin' from a basic prototype into a fully functional tool.

Progress

V5: Current Version

For the final proof of concept version of MapPin, I combined the reliability and low tolerances of Lego parts with custom 3D printed components. This allowed me to create a tactile map with a 4x4 resolution, providing a tactile experience.

V4: Quick MVP

For my next version, I wanted to get to an MVP as soon as possible so that I could user test this. I decided to rig an existing pin-art toy to some servos. By removing the plexiglass and grouping pins using plates, I could rotate the servos attached to a helix structure to drive the pins up.

I knew the 4x5 setup was low resolution and the 10-second update time was slow, but it was sufficient to demonstrate the concept.

Input Processing

Tracking user movements accurately over small distances is crucial. Public GPS is not accurate enough, so I used an MPU9205 9-axis IMU.

simply integrating angular accelerations of the gyro worked for heading, but displacement was noisy. I looked into Kalman Filters and the Madgwick Algorithm to clean this data.

Currently, I have placed a button that, when pressed, simulates walking forward a small distance, which is sufficient for a proof of concept.

Path Planning & Map Conversion

Path Planning: I implemented a Breadth First Search to find the shortest path from the user (Blue Circle) to the target. This removes irrelevant roads from the 3D rendering. I explored EBS-A* for future optimization on primitive hardware.

Map Conversion: Instead of proprietary maps, I intend for users to feed in a map of the area. My software currently uses a simple algorithm to check pixel color intensity to identify paths. For complex maps like theme parks, I plan to take user input to highlight paths, avoiding the heavy resource cost of training large CNN models.

Software & Hardware Challenges

I created a software architecture to handle logical interactions. On the hardware side, I tested low-power solenoids but found they drew too much current (0.5 amps) and overheated. Staggered power cycles didn't solve the issue, leading me to reconsider the hardware approach.

V3: Pivoting Approach

After speaking to a PhD student at MIT, I learned that dynamic pins might not be as valuable if users don't care about the specific obstruction type (tree vs building). I experimented with constraining the problem.

I tried low-voltage solenoid pistons to simplify the design, accepting that the 3D representation resolution would decrease. I realized that reaching a true MVP with 9g servos was physically impossible due to wire management and update cycle latency.

However, the multiplexed servo system worked! I could host a web server and establish a websocket connection with the app, marking the proof of concept as a success.

V1: CAD Design

My first version was originally made in CAD, featuring 24 9g servos arranged to actuate the pins. This laid the groundwork for understanding the spatial constraints and mechanical requirements of the device.
